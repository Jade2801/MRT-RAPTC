Overview

Psychological skills training (PST) is the “systematic and consistent practice of mental or psychological skills for the purpose of enhancing performance, increasing enjoyment, or achieving greater sport or physical satisfaction”. PST tends to be neglected because of myths built around it such as: it is from problem individuals only, elite athletes only, quick fix solution and it isn’t useful. PST helps increase all eight pillars of MRT. Multiple studies have found that PST has apositive effect on performance. There are four steps to the implementation of a PST program: education phase, acquisition phase, practice phase and self-regulation phase.

The US Olympic committee gave top 10 principles for PST (these are relavant fro the RAPTC):

  1. Mental training can’t replace physical training
  2. Physical training and physical ability alone are not enough to succeed consistently at elite levels
  3. A strong mind may not win an Olympic medal but a weak one will lose it
  4. Coaches/Instructors will not frequently know what their athletes are thinking
  5. Thoughts affect behaviour, therefore consistent thoughts will result in consistent behaviour
  6. Coaches have a different view of changing technical mistakes vs mental mistakes
  7. Coaches must be involved in the psychological/ mental training
  8. There is a need to force athletes to put time and effort into mental training
  9. Mental skills should be measured in order to maximise performance
  10. Coaches need to think about their own mental skills

A randomised control study found that between a control group of soldiers receiving no mental training and a group of soldiers receiving psychological skills training, the latter reported a greater use of cognitive skills, increased self-belief and performed significantly better on a range of different military task including the fitness related tasks

The below sections will give you more information on each psychological skill.

Motivation

Motivation is the ability to initiate and persist at a task.

Positive motivation can be attained through the satisfaction of three psychological needs:

  • Autonomy: feeling empowered and having a choice.
  • Competence: Feeling capable and effective.
  • Competence: Feeling valued by- and close to- others.

Arousal Regulation

Arousal is a “state of physiological and psychological activation that varies on a continuum from deep sleep to intense excitement”. Anxiety is a “negative emotional state characterised by feelings of nervousness and worry associated with activation or arousal of the body (e.g.: becoming nervous from increased heart rate)”. Arousal influences performance, it can increase muscle tension and coordination difficulties, it can also impact attention and concentration.

There are many arousal regulation techniques, there are two types: techniques to reduce arousal and techniques to increase arousal. This is an arousal reducing technique:

  • Breathing techniques: it is one of the easiest and most effective ways to control anxiety and muscle tension. Observing someone’s breathing gives information on their state of arousal. If someone is calm, confident and in control their breathing will be smooth, deep and rhythmical. When someone is under pressure and tense, their breathing will be short, shallow, and irregular.

However, not all physical and mental tension is negative and not all of it should be eliminated. Small amounts of tension are desirable when a state of alertness and attentiveness is required. These are a few signs of under-arousal: moving slowly, easily distracted, lack of concern about performance, lack of enthusiasm and anticipation and heavy legs. The following techniques are used to enhance/induce arousal:

  • Increase breathing rate
  • Act energised
  • Listen to music

Self-efficacy

Self-efficacy also known as self-confidence is the degree of certainty one possesses about their ability to be successful. There are multiple positive effects of self-confidence: it arouses positive emotions, facilitates concentration, positively affects goals, increases effort, positively affects psychological momentum . If an individual lacks necessary skill, has insufficient motivation, or the task is ambiguous, then self-efficacy will not lead to desired performance. However, if someone has the skills and motivation, then self-efficacy is a robust predictor of performance.

Self-confidence is influenced by the following:

  • Performance accomplishments "I have done it before so I can do it again"
    Past performance accomplishments have the most impact on one's self-confidence when an individual successfully completes a difficult task without external assistance or experiences rare failure.
  • Vicarious experiences: "If she can do it, I can do it"
    It is important to watch others perform the task or skill that one is trying to learn. This will work if the skills and attributes fo the performers are very similar.
  • Persuasion: "I know you are a good athlete"
    Techniques for persuasion include verbal persuasion, evaluative feedback, expectations from others and self-talk. Persuasion needs to be used with other techniques; it is harder to instil strong self-efficacy beliefs with only persuasion techniques.
  • Physiological states: "My fast heart-beat signifies excitement"
    The individual’s perception of their own physiological state has an important impact on their self-efficacy.

Imagery

Imagery also known as visualisation or mental practice involves creating or recreating an experience in one’s mind, using all the senses as well as moods and emotions.
Why practice imagery ? These are its positive outcomes:

  • Improve concentration
  • Increase and build confidence
  • Emotional control
  • Acquire and practice certain skills and strategies
  • Cope with pain and injury
  • Solve strategy, technique or psychological problems

PETTLEP is a good model to implement imagery:

  • Physical: Image the relevant physical characteristics e.g., a soldier with their kit
  • Environment: Relevant details of the environment need to be included in the imagery process e.g., warfare noise
  • Task: Image the details relevant to the task and the level so that the attentional demands and situation changes are the same in the imaged and actual situation
  • Timing: Image in real time, but "slow/fast motion" can be used to emphasise different skills
  • Learning: The imagery should evolve and adapt with learnign and practice
  • Emotion: The same emotions experienced during the physical experience should be included in the imaging
  • Perspective: either internal (through your eyes) or external (through someone else's eyes)

Goal Setting

There are 3 types of goals:

  • Outcome goals focus on a result and should facilitate short-term motivation BUT can increase anxiety and distracting thoughts e.g., "I want to get into the RAPTC; therefore, I have to pass the selection course and be one of the best".
  • Performance goals focus on achieving standards or performance objectives without considering competitiors e.g., "I need to pass the RFTS to stay on the course".
  • Process goals focus on the actions needed to perform well e.g., "I need to have good technique while doing the deadlift to do it successfully".
Process and performance goals can make more precise adjustments to goals and depend less on others' behaviours.

FUNDAMENTAL aspects of Goal Setting:
Difficult goals lead to higher levels of performance than easier goals and specific goals lead to higher levels of performance than vague goals.
Short term goals are more effective than long term goals: they offer more opportunities to assess/correct. Long term goals should be used with short term goals to mark progress.

Set SMART goals:

  • Specific (What do you want to do?)
  • Measurable (How will you know when you've reached it?)
  • Achievable (Is it in your power to accomplish it?)
  • Realistic: (Can you realistically achieve it ?)
  • Timely: (When do you want to accomplish it?)

Focus regulation

Improving attention is necessary for optimal performance. There are multiple different techniques to promote concentration, combat lapses of attention and avoid distractions.

To improve concentration skills, it is important for individual to learn how to “park” their intruding thoughts, learn to maintain focus, rehearse concentration in high-pressured environments and learn to shift their attention.

Dealing with pain or discomfort from PT:

  • Dissociative strategy: distracting one’s attention from pain and discomfort by focusing on external factors e.g., listening to music, concentrating on one’s surroundings.
  • Associative strategy: focusing on the information from own body and monitoring the pain. It is about fraternising with pain/discomfort and understanding how to increase one’s tolerance to it.

Relaxation

Being relaxed is a key characteristic of peak performance. There are 3 types of relaxation: physical, mental and multimodal:

  • Physical relaxation: Progressive muscle relaxation is used to relieve muscle tension. Decreased muscle tension will lead to decreased mental tension. It is a systematic focus of attention on the various gross muscle groups throughout the body.
  • Mental relaxation: Mental relaxation can be obtained by using a technique known as transcendental meditation, it is associated with slower heart rate, lower blood pressure and decreased responsivity of the sympathetic nervous system.
  • Multimodal relaxation: Both physical and mental relaxation, these techniques deal with both types of anxiety simultaneously.

Self-talk

Self-talk is described as the act of talking to oneself, aloud or silently. Self-talk has many potential uses such as enhancing concentration, breaking bad habits, stopping negative thoughts, staying motivated, sustaining effort, enhancing self-confidence , … There are different types of self-talk: positive (motivational), instructional, negative self-talk or thought stopping :

  • Negativeself-talk tends to be associated with poor performance. However, negative self-talk can have positive impacts on performance when used appropriately (i.e., it can be interpreted as motivational, e.g., “a talking to” following a mistake).
    Examples: "That was a stupid shot", "Why are you so bad at this"
  • Thought-stoppingself-talk is useful to stop negative thoughts before they harm performance, it is often associated with a trigger or a cue work to stop a negative thought and clear mind to refocus on a task-related cue.
    Examples: "Stop!", "Not now!", "Focus!"
  • Positiveself-talk can either involve motivational elements or substitute negative talk for positive (e.g., I’m going to fail -> I can do this).
    Examples: "I can do it", "Just hang on in there a little longer"
  • Instructionalself-talk is used to perform a skill as well as possible (e.g., when performing a squat, it is important to know all the queues and coaching points and to be able to talk them through to oneself).
    "Keep your eyes on the ball", "Push your knees out"

Guide to self-talk: the 4Ws
Where? Training and events
When? Pre-event, during event, during training
Why? Skill execution & development and to enhance focus, self-confidence, drive as well as regulate arousal
What? Structure, task instruction, nature of task
